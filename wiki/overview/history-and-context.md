# History and Context

## Retrieved timeline
- November 2004: the sample website’s patient-safety page says Microsoft began working with the NHS in England through the NHS Common User Interface programme.
- 14 March 2007: Digital Health reported that EMIS intended to incorporate CUI elements into EMIS Web and that INPS was also engaging with the guidance.
- 10 September 2007: Microsoft publicly launched Health CUI as free guidance plus toolkit code.
- 10 September 2007: a same-day Digital Health report said NHS adoption was still limited and framed EMIS as the largest supplier then beginning to incorporate CUI elements.
- 13 May 2008: Microsoft announced Health CUI version 1.3 with substantial guidance updates and an 18-month roadmap.
- 2008: System C said Medway Sigma had become the first trust-wide UK product available to standardize on the new CUI.
- 2008: the Patient Journey Demonstrator article described a Silverlight exemplar implementation intended to showcase MSCUI guidance.
- 13 July 2009: Digital Health reported Microsoft partner recognition for EMIS and CareWorks and referenced an iLink HealthVault/CUI implementation.
- 27 November 2009: Digital Health reported Aintree's trust-wide use of System C Medway Sigma and said the suite incorporated significant CUI elements.
- 13 February 2012: Indra said Valencia's ABUCASIS modernization adapted Microsoft Health Common User Interface principles.
- 2010: NHS retrospective sources say nine CUI standards were published.
- 2010: archived ISB release pages and DSCN documents show the formal publication route for ISB 1500-1508.
- 2015: later NHS requirements documents still referenced CUI standards and guidance as active requirements.
- 4 July 2019: NHS Digital formally deprecated the CUI standards.
- 2024: a published HCI-modelling paper explicitly combined NHS CUI with the NHS Design System.

## Retrieved programme framing
- Microsoft and NHS sources consistently frame the programme around patient safety, clinician effectiveness, consistency across systems, and reduced training burden.
- The sample website states that design guidance was produced through user-centred design, usability testing, consultation with software providers, and integrated hazard assessments.

## Synthesis
- The programme sits at the intersection of design standards work, software toolkit delivery, and NHS clinical-safety ambitions during the NHS Connecting for Health era.
- The corpus shows a shift from early ASP.NET AJAX and WinForms controls toward WPF and Silverlight-era demonstrators, which helps explain some dated technical assumptions in the UI implementations.

## Modern interpretation
- Historically, Health CUI is best understood as a serious attempt to standardize high-risk clinical interactions rather than as a generic enterprise design system.
- Today, the programme is most valuable as a historical safety corpus and pattern library, especially for desktop-style clinician workflows and dense information environments.
- The afterlife evidence suggests partial adoption and long shadow rather than universal standardization: the patient banner and demographic standards endured most clearly.
- The newer supplier evidence sharpens the middle ground: this was neither purely aspirational guidance nor a fully universal NHS standard. It had real deployment footprints, but those footprints still look uneven and only partially provable.

## Uncertainty
- Parts of the original `cui.nhs.uk` programme site are now localized from the National Archives, and the surviving replay perimeter currently looks small; one discovered `Default.aspx` path still remains unresolved behind a fresh archive challenge.
- The CodePlex release channel is now much better reconstructed, but release attachments and browseable source contents are still incomplete because direct attachment URLs currently reject unauthenticated access.
