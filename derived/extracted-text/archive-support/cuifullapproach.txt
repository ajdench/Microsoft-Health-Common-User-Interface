   Purpose of the document


   This document outlines the delivery lifecycle applicable to the work of the Clinical Applications and
   Patient Safety (CAPS) Project, which forms a part of the Department of Health Informatics
   Directorate (DHID), Common User Interface (CUI) programme. The lifecycle intends to draw
   together the multiple stakeholders involved in the work of CAPS and identify interdependencies
   between them. In particular, this document describes the integration of NHS Clinical Safety and the
   NHS Information Standards activities, in order to satisfy both safety requirements, authorities, and
   to gain successful standards submissions through the Information Standards Board for Health and
   Social Care (ISB HaSC).


   The document also serves as a supplementary source of generic process information, in support of
   each of the standards submitted to the ISB HaSC, where a single source of generic cross-standard
   information better suits the purposes of submissions, than duplication of information across the
   submissions.


   All new versions of this document will be referenced in subsequent submissions to the ISB HaSC.
   Section 4 of this document contains the information necessary to support the Evaluation
   requirements of an ISB HaSC submission.




   Intended Audience


   This document is primarily aimed at a group of those stakeholders who currently interconnect with
   the CAPS Project, including the ISB HaSC, DHID Clinical Safety Group (CSG), the NHS National
   Integration Centre (NIC), Local Service Providers (LSPs), National Application Service Providers
   (NASPs) Independent Software Vendors (ISVs), and Microsoft.


   It is assumed, therefore, that the representatives to whom this document is circulated to will have
   knowledge of the NHS, Department of Health Informatics Directorate (DHID), the history of the
   National Programme for IT (NPfIT), impact of the National Local Ownership Programme (NLOP)
   and the Regional Programmes for IT that have evolved from this, as well as the high-level
   organisational structure of the Department of Health (DH). Where relevant, references to related
   documents will be given for further reading.

The CUI Programme is a funded programme within DHID. Phase 2 of the programme is split into
five Projects:


      1. Clinical Applications and Patient Safety (CAPS) [previously “Design Guide” and “Toolkit”]
      2. Information Management and Technology (IM&T) Tools
      3. Knowledge Worker Tools (KWT)
      4. NHS Clinicians Portal
      5. NHSmail Technical Refresh (MTR)


The lifecycle outlined in this document covers the design guidance outputs from the CAPS Project
only.


The high-level objective of the CAPS Project is to establish user interface design guidelines,
standards and associated technical components for the high-priority, safety-critical areas of clinical
functionality contained, or planned to be contained, within clinical systems provided to the NHS in
England.


The remit of the output from the CAPS Project within the CUI programme is described in each
Standard Submission as "clinical systems within the NHS in England within the remit of NHS
Connecting for Health”.


Clinical systems within the context of the CAPS Project are defined as all those software
applications within the contractual control of NHS Connecting for Health used in the processing of
patient identifiable data. This includes those clinical systems delivered within the scope of the
existing LSP and National Application Service Provider (NASP) contracts.


It is recommended that all other suppliers of clinical systems that include CUI standards move
towards implementing them (once they are approved) as part of their updating and maintenance
programmes.


For more information on the CUI programme, please contact the CUI                            team    via
cuistakeholder.mailbox@nhs.net or visit the programme website at www.cui.nhs.uk

Design Guidance Development Lifecycle
 Overview


Figure 1 on the following page provides a diagram outlining the development lifecycle. Due to the
size of the diagram it is best to read it in electronic format. It is possible, however, to make some
general comments regarding the lifecycle, which are made below. A *.vsd file is provided here
containing the diagram in order to enable detailed viewing.




Developed upon existing processes
The lifecycle consists of five stages: Envisioning, Planning, Development, Stabilisation and Live.
These stages are derived from Microsoft‟s Solutions Framework (MSF), as much of the
development of the design guidance (and solutions toolkit) will be carried out in partnership with
Microsoft, the DHID development partner working on the CUI programme. Other established
processes integrated into the lifecycle are derived as below:


        The Clinical / Patient Safety activities referenced in this document are taken from the
        Clinical Safety Management System as defined for suppliers of systems to the NHS under
        the NPfIT (see document references 1 and 2);


        The Information Standards process for developing standards is taken from existing
        templates for Operational Standards developed by the ISB HaSC (see document
        references 4-6).


Releases and work items
The CUI programme will run over two phases, the first of which was covered by the previous
version of this lifecycle document. This version of the document has been updated for the second
phase of the programme running from October 2007 until December 2009.


The second phase is broken down into several releases, of anything between four and nine
months in duration, dependent on the needs of the work area being covered in a given release.
This lifecycle document describes activities which occur in relation to a single release. Each CAPS
release has several work items (eg. Medication Line, Prescribing, Date and Time controls, and a
Clinical Terminology user interface) which may result in the production of a Design Guide entry.

The activities listed in the lifecycle are either applicable across all work items in a release, or a
single work item. This differentiation is indicated by the „light‟, „medium‟ and „heavy‟ rating
respectively. In any one release it is likely that different work items will be at different stages of
maturity. It is realistic, for example, to be developing a piece of design work on medications
management (Development stage) whilst carrying out a pilot of Date and Time input controls
implemented in a test system (Stabilisation stage).


Some of the larger areas of scope within the CAPS Project (for example, medications
management) are likely to require multiple releases of development to achieve a level of maturity
satisfactory to stakeholders and suitable to publish as a complete Design Guide entry.


The general landscape
The majority of activities are carried out by Microsoft and the DHID CAPS team. This is greatest
between the Envisioning and Development stages. There is little activity from Microsoft in
Stabilisation and Live once they have delivered the Design Guidance. In these stages the focus
shifts to the DHID CAPS Project team and other key stakeholders.


In all stages there is an important integration with the Clinical Safety Group, who assures the
clinical safety work being carried out. Similarly, The ISB HaSC assurance activities play a key role
from Planning to Live assessing the rigour of the CAPS work as pieces of design guidance are
submitted to become standards. Attaining Clinical Safety sign off on these work items is a pre-
cursor to delivering a Full Standard. Both the Clinical Safety Group and ISB HaSC have a key role
to play during Stabilisation in determining the required level of implementation of a piece of design
guidance in order to attain Draft and Full Standards approvals, explained in more detail later on.

   Compliance


    Commercial Issues
   The relevant Intellectual Property Rights, which might concern the use of the
   proposed standard within the NHS, are owned jointly by NHS Connecting for Health
   and Microsoft. This is defined in the Development Agreement, Clause 13,
   "Intellectual Property Rights".
   The Development Agreement is an exhibit of the Contract between the Authority
   (NHS) and the Company (Microsoft), signed December 2005 and renewed in
   October 2007.
   Design Guidance and Standards are available on a royalty free licence basis to any
   and all suppliers to the NHS in England, as well as to other national and international
   clinical systems suppliers and health procurement agencies, as long as such use is
   in line with the terms and conditions defined in the Participation Agreement (where
   suppliers or other agents take an active role in the development process) or in the
   End User Licence Agreement, the terms of which must be accepted prior to access
   being granted to the CUI web site where all guidance will be published.
   The CUI development agreement with Microsoft will continue to provide support and
   development of guidance and standards to end December 2009. Ongoing
   maintenance costs in relation to guidance and standards will be provided for beyond
   this date within the operating budget of the DHID Technology Office.

    Strategic fit
   The CUI programme forms a component of the Technology Office strategy to deliver
   and support the goals and objectives of the NPfIT. The resultant CUI CAPS
   Information Standards will continue to support the delivery of this agreed national
   programme of work involving the necessary use of information technology, through a
   process of clinical systems review against the core objectives of the NPfIT and DHID:

           Increased patient safety;
           Increased clinical take up of systems;
           Increased clinician effectiveness and efficiency;
           Reduced training costs.

    Operational Fit
   The proposed standards will cover the human interface of clinical systems only. They
   do not relate to, and as such will not cover underlying processing or data storage.

   Operationally it is envisaged that clinical system developers will use the standards in
   two ways:
      1. When evaluating an existing system against the standard, developers will
          make a direct comparison against the existing designs and that defined in the
          standards;
       2. When commissioning and designing a new system the standard would be
          applied early in the user interface design process.


   Currently all LSPs and NASPs are in the process of developing the systems which
   will enable access to electronic patient records. These systems may implement
   design solutions which diverge from the proposed standards. Please see sections 6.7
   Migration Plans and 7 Maintenance for further detail on how a plan for this will be
   developed.

    Interdependencies
   There are a range of interdependencies which have been evaluated and documented
   for each standard. The CAPS team has reviewed relevant known standards. It is
   envisaged that none of those reviewed and listed in each submission will prevent
   progression of standards through the ISB HaSC process at this stage. In the event of
   issues arising, the CAPS team will work with the relevant body to resolve any issues.
   This may result in updates to CUI CAPS standards.


    Governance
   Identification of governance issues relating to clinical safety, patient confidentiality,
   data security, etc. will be included in hazard assessment workshops and safety
   reviews, as well as a part of the clinical user testing process.

   Accredited clinicians will be used to either host or take part in hazard assessments
   against each of the clinical areas covered by the design guidance in development.
   This will ensure trained specialists are involved in the process, with expertise in the
   identification of these types of issue.

   In addition to this, the design guide team will develop and review design guidance in
   development against all known and relevant bodies, codes of practice, and standards
   relevant to the clinical area being addressed. This will ensure adherence to the
   expected level of rigour within DHID programmes.

    Provisional testing approach
   As outlined in the section regarding the general lifecycle each stage of development
   has been subject to iterative testing and validation from a clinical, user and patient
   safety perspective. An DHID Hazard Log is available for reference. This records
   patient safety risks and mitigations throughout the design process. In terms of user
   engagement, there are two levels.

   During Development end users are involved in much of the user research and user
   testing undertaken. Additionally relevant Royal Colleges and Professional Regulatory
   bodies will be consulted across the CUI programme.

   During the pilot stage further clinical user testing and patient safety analysis will be
   undertaken. The results will be used in analysis of the designs proposed and used in
   rework where applicable. This may also result in general recommendations for
   human behavioural, organisational and technical user guidance to be given by
   suppliers.

   The NIC also plays a key role in the technical assurance of design guidance, and this
   activity begins at the end of Development and continues through to the Live stage.
   This is linked to system suppliers implementing design guidance, which occurs
   during Stabilisation and Live. The suppliers are also involved in the CAPS usability
   assessments which are carried out during Development.

   Supplier testing will be carried out as part of their standard release process. This will
   cover clinical and functional testing. Specific human behavioural, organisational and
   technical user guidance will be given by the application supplier to their users as part
   of the standard release process for that supplier.

   High level performance characteristics are specified within each standard as a first
   point of reference for testing criteria.

    Compliance Testing
   A set of compliance criteria will be developed to enable the NIC to assess adherence
   with the CUI CAPS standards across LSP hosted systems. These will be applied to
   systems as and when they enter the test environments in the NIC following the
   required compliance date for a given standard.

   These tests will be objective, measurable and quantifiable, and will reflect the specific
   requirement of each given piece of design guidance. These tests will be based on a
   mix of software tests and human evaluations, as per existing NIC processes.
   Compliance will be required for these systems based on the commitment to be
   compliant with all current data standards within the LSP contracts.

   The compliance criteria will also be made available to any systems suppliers wishing

   Maintenance
   Once a Design Guide entry or a Full Information Standard is published in the public
   domain and is implemented by suppliers, a system will be in place to manage the
   ongoing ownership of and any arising problems with the standard.

   There will be two forms of maintenance which are described below.

    Reactive maintenance
   During the life of the CUI programme it is possible that a supplier may contend the
   viability of a piece of CAPS guidance/standard, or may present an equally clinically
   safe alternative solution. It is important therefore that a feedback loop is set-up
   between compliance testing processes at the NIC and the CAPS team, where the
   CAPS team are consulted on either the clinical evidence of alternate designs or to
   provide feedback to suppliers on design rationale, if appropriate.

   The CAPS Project will publish only a single standard for each aspect of the interface;
   however this may not be the best outcome for the NHS, so a review mechanism with
   links to the ISB HaSC will need to consider any such issues as they arise. Any
   significant changes to a standard will need assurance of the likes of that proposed in
   the above lifecycle.

   There is currently a three yearly cycle of standards review undertaken by the ISB
   HaSC. Sufficient resource and funding will be available within the Technology Office,
   such that if review of any CUI CAPS Full Standard results in a need for rework of the
   standard, a work package can be defined and team assembled to ensure the work is
   completed to update the standard accordingly.

   For clinical incidents that occur within a trust that relate to a piece of CAPS design

    Proactive maintenance
   In addition to the reactive approach above, CAPS is taking a proactive approach
   during the life of the CUI development programme to identifying issues with usability,
   interoperability, implementability and clinical safety by approaching system suppliers
   and system users for evidence. This may take the form of reviewing a supplier‟s
   hazard log or interviewing technical staff in a trust. There are many techniques for
   information extraction. All evidence gathered will then feed into a standards design
   review which will occur either during the 3 yearly cycle of review based on the ISB
   HaSC review periods for existing standards, or for those areas requiring urgent
   resolution, on the basis of best endeavours resolution in a shorter timescale as
   required.

   In terms of versioning, it is suggested that small incremental versions are released
   where a design fix has been provided due to a clinical incident. During each three
   yearly review, if there are significant changes to a standard based on evidence
   gathered proactively, then a more significant increment is probable. In all cases,
   changes to the standard will be reviewed by the ISB HaSC for approval prior to
   publishing.

    Ownership
   DHID will maintain ownership of the standards within the Technology Office, under
   the Data Standards and Products (DS&P) Directorate. For the duration of the CUI
   programme, the CAPS Project team within DS&P will provide the single point of
   contact in relation to any issues or amendments arising out of Live implementations
   in the NHS.

   Once the CUI CAPS project is completed the Business As Usual (BAU) team within
   the Data Standards and Products Directorate will form the first point of contact for
   any questions and queries about CUI CAPS standards.

   CUI CAPS Clinical Safety Approach
   The CUI CAPS project has developed their clinical safety management approach
   based on the Clinical Safety Management System (CSMS) prescribed to system
   suppliers by the DHID and the emerging information standard, “Application of Clinical
   Risk Management to the Manufacture of Health Software.”

   Although outputs from the CUI CAPS project, namely text based user interface
   design guidance, are not systems per se, they will inform the user interface work
   carried out by clinical system suppliers which will in turn end up being part of a live
   deployed system. It is crucial, therefore, that the outputs from CUI CAPS are also
   clinically safe.

   The DHID CSMS outlines four risk areas: End to end clinical process risk; Technical
   risk; Message risk; and Patient safety risk. As the CAPS project is focused on the
   user interface layer of an application, the scope of the CAPS safety process is limited
   to patient safety risks. The other risk types are classed out of CUI scope. However,
   where related risks are raised, CUI CAPS will record and transfer those risks to the
   appropriate parties.

   As the CUI CAPS Project is responsible for developing user interface design
   guidelines which will be utilised within clinical applications - not the development of
   applications themselves – the CUI CAPS Clinical Safety Cases are developed as
   Pre-Operational. Taking a typical software development lifecycle (see the table
   below), the scope of CUI design guidance covers the stages of Define Requirements,
   Design and Develop. As such, the CUI CAPS Clinical Safety Cases will cover these
   stages also (although it important to note that the Patient Safety Assessment‟s
   themselves are very much focused upon context of use). It is the intention that the
   CUI CAPS safety documentation will be the starting point for NHS clinical application
   developers upon which to build their own Clinical Safety Case and, ultimately, pass
   on to end users within the live estate.

   In order to build the Clinical Safety Case, each CUI CAPS design guide will undergo
   an end to end Patient Safety Assessment (PSA). It is important to note that a Patient
   Safety Assessment (PSA) is not a distinct activity, scheduled into the development
   lifecycle as a milestone. A PSA is the term used to describe the end to end,
   continuous, iterative assessment to establish the safety integrity of the deliverable. It
   is integrated into the development lifecycle for each CAPS deliverable right from the
   outset.

   A CUI CAPS PSA consists of two main „processes‟: Hazard identification and risk
   assessment; and Hazard analysis and risk management. Various safety specific
   activities (eg. risk assessment workshop), methods (eg. Structured What If
   Technique, Hierarchical Task Analysis) and tools (eg. hazard checklists, cause and
   effect (Fishbone) diagrams) can be used during a PSA. However, all usual activities
   undertaken as part of normal research and development processes for CUI CAPS,
   ie. non safety specific, will pro-actively elicit patient safety issues and hazards on an
   ongoing basis. These are then fed into the master Hazard Log via the Clinical Safety
   Advisor for management.

   As part of an end to end Patient Safety Assessment (PSA), the following safety
   specific activities should take place for each CUI CAPS deliverable (design guide):

           Each CAPS deliverable should have an assessment during the scoping
           phase to identify the minimum number and type of safety specific activities to
           take place and when.
           As a minimum, each CAPS deliverable should have:
           o   A web based survey, including safety specific questions.
           o   One to one interviews with representative end users, including safety
               specific questions.
           o   Patient Safety Risk Assessment (PSA) workshops, eg.
                1 x Opening Risk Assessment workshop with representative end users
                 (pre design: safety requirements and objectives and preliminary
                 hazard identification and risk assessment).
                1 x Closing Risk Assessment workshop with representative end users
                 (post design: risk identification, grading and management).
                Interim Risk Assessments can take place as required, such as when a
                 design guide spans multiple releases.
           The purpose of an Opening Risk Assessment is to inform the CUI CAPS
           requirements process (including safety requirements). Very often, the ORA
           can raise hazards and issues that are either out of scope or not patient safety
           specific – as it is conducted during the very early, conceptual stages of
           design.

           The purpose of a Closing (or Interim) Risk Assessment is to formally risk
